# W3.HedgeDensity

**Spec:** When the hedge-word density in the deliverable's analysis body exceeds a configurable threshold (default: 6% of total non-stop-word tokens), the Critic emits a structured warning naming the hedge-word count, total token count, density percentage, and a sample of the most-frequent hedge phrases. Hedge words are tracked against a curated catalog of legal-hedge vocabulary (`may`, `could`, `arguably`, `potentially`, `it appears`, `it would seem`, `perhaps`, `possibly`, `to some extent`, `generally speaking`, `the better view is`, `most courts would likely`, etc.).
**Enforcement point:** The Critic sub-agent runs the hedge-density computation in its post-Drafter pass. The warning is added to the deliverable's `warnings[]` block.
**Severity:** warning.
**Failure mode:** Deliverable is annotated. The reviewer sees: "⚠️ Hedge density 8.2% (threshold 6%). Most-frequent hedges: 'may' (12), 'could' (8), 'arguably' (5). Consider whether the underlying uncertainty warrants R11 LowConfidenceGate rather than in-line hedging."

## Detail

W3 is the rule that catches a specific LLM tic: when the model is uncertain about something, it tends to hedge in prose rather than escalate. A deliverable with 30 instances of "may" and "could" sprinkled through it sounds appropriately cautious, but the cumulative effect is to make the conclusion non-actionable. The reviewer doesn't know whether to trust any specific sentence.

W3 surfaces the hedge density numerically so the reviewer can make an informed call: is this analysis genuinely about a doctrinally uncertain area (and the hedges are appropriate), or is the agent hedging because it's not confident in its retrievals (in which case R11 should have fired)?

**Hedge catalog.** The catalog of hedge phrases is curated and lives at `extensions/agentic-law-os/w3-config.json`. Initial categories:

- **Modal hedges:** may, could, might, possibly, perhaps
- **Epistemic hedges:** it appears, it would seem, it is arguable, one could argue
- **Frequency hedges:** generally, typically, in most cases, usually, often
- **Authority hedges:** most courts would likely, the better view is, the majority view is
- **Outcome hedges:** there is a strong argument that, a court might conclude, depending on the facts

The catalog distinguishes legitimate hedges (an area is genuinely doctrinally split: "courts are split on whether...") from low-content hedges (filler uncertainty: "the worker could be classified as..."). The distinction is by frequency and context; high-density of low-content hedges is the W3 fire signal.

**Density calculation.** `count(hedge_tokens) / count(non_stop_word_tokens_in_analysis_body)`. The analysis body is the deliverable's main analysis section, not the entire deliverable. Citation strings, the classification overlay, and the verify-flags block are excluded from both numerator and denominator. This focuses the measurement on the actual prose where hedging matters.

**Why the threshold is 6%.** Empirically derived from a survey of well-written legal memos: experienced attorneys hedge at ~3–5% in analytical prose. Going to 6% indicates either the topic is genuinely uncertain (acceptable) or the agent is over-hedging (problematic). The W3 fire surfaces the density; the reviewer's job is to judge which case applies.

**What to do when W3 fires.** The reviewer's options:

1. **Accept and acknowledge:** the underlying topic is genuinely doctrinally uncertain. Acceptable; deliverable proceeds with the warning visible.
2. **Force the agent to pick a position:** the hedging is excessive. The reviewer rejects the draft and asks the agent to take a clearer position with named confidence (HIGH/MEDIUM) instead of in-line hedges.
3. **Escalate to R11:** the hedging masks insufficient research. The reviewer asks "you're hedging because the corpus is thin?" and triggers a re-research with broader retrieval.

**Distinction from R11.** R11 LowConfidenceGate is a structural confidence gate (rule-based, mechanical). W3 is a prose-level density check. They can co-fire, and when they do, R11 wins (W3's warning becomes part of the incomplete-response).

**False-positive mitigation.** Some legitimate legal-research prose has higher hedge density — e.g., a question about a circuit-split topic naturally requires "the Second Circuit holds X, but the Ninth Circuit holds Y, and a court in our jurisdiction could go either way." W3's threshold is set high enough that legitimate hedging in genuinely split areas doesn't trigger; only excessive hedging does. The Critic's accompanying text indicates which hedges drove the warning, so the reviewer can verify.

## Interactions with other rules

- **R11 LowConfidenceGate** is the structural gate; W3 is the prose-level annotation. Both can fire.
- **R13 VerifyFlagCap** governs structured verify flags; W3 governs prose hedges. Both are uncertainty surfacers but at different layers.
- **W2 ParaphraseDriftCheck** is about drift between revisions; W3 is about hedge density within a single draft. Independent.

## Test cases

✅ No warning: 4% density in genuinely complex analysis
```
Analysis body: 1200 non-stop-word tokens. Hedge tokens: 48. Density: 4.0%.
Threshold: 6%. W3 does not fire.
```

⚠️ Warning fires: 8.5% density
```
Analysis body: 800 tokens. Hedge tokens: 68. Density: 8.5%.
Most frequent: "may" (14), "could" (11), "arguably" (8), "potentially" (6), "perhaps" (5).
W3 fires. Annotation: "⚠️ Hedge density 8.5% (threshold 6%). Most-frequent hedges: 'may' (14), 'could' (11), 'arguably' (8). Sample sentence: 'The worker may potentially be classified as an employee, although arguably the ABC test could apply.' Consider whether R11 LowConfidenceGate is the right surface for the underlying uncertainty rather than in-line hedging."
```

✅ No warning: legitimate circuit-split prose at 7% but contextually justified
```
Analysis body: 1500 tokens. Hedge tokens: 105. Density: 7.0%.
W3 fires (over 6% threshold). Annotation includes hedge breakdown. Reviewer reads: hedges are clustered around the words "Second Circuit" and "Ninth Circuit" — clearly handling a circuit split. Accepts the deliverable with the warning visible.
```

⚠️ Warning fires: hedge clustering on a single claim
```
Analysis body: 600 tokens. Total hedge density: 5.5% (under threshold).
But: one claim (50 tokens) has 8 hedges (16% density). Critic flags this as a per-claim hedge concentration.
W3 fires with a refinement: "⚠️ Hedge density elevated in claim #3 (16% local density, 5.5% overall). The claim may need stronger retrieval or escalation to R11 specifically for that point."
(This per-claim refinement is a Phase 4 enhancement; v1 uses overall density only.)
```

✅ No warning: deliverable proceeds with low hedging
```
Analysis body: 700 tokens. Hedge tokens: 21. Density: 3.0%.
Below threshold; no W3.
```

## Open questions

- **Threshold tuning.** 6% is the starting threshold. Calibrate against ~50 production deliverables.
- **Per-claim density.** Concentrated hedging in a single claim is a stronger signal than diffuse hedging. v1 measures overall; Phase 4 may add per-claim refinement.
- **Legitimate-hedge whitelisting.** Some hedges are appropriate in specific structural positions: e.g., "could argue X but Y" introducing a counter-argument is fine. Worth pattern-matching for legitimate hedge structures? Phase 4 design.
- **Cross-language behavior.** All hedge catalog entries are English. If the agent is asked to produce a non-English deliverable (Phase 7+), W3 needs per-language catalogs. Out of v1 scope.
