# R13.VerifyFlagCap

**Spec:** The Drafter may emit at most 3 `verify_flag[]` entries per deliverable. A `verify_flag` is a structured pointer (`{ topic, location_in_draft, why_flagged, suggested_verification }`) marking a specific assertion the human reviewer should independently verify. More than 3 flags indicates the research is too uncertain for a deliverable; the orchestrator escalates to R11 LowConfidenceGate.
**Enforcement point:** Drafter output schema (`schemas/claim.schema.json` for the parent draft schema; `schemas/deliverable.schema.json` for the rendered deliverable) caps `verify_flags[].length` at 3. Schema violations trigger a `revise` action; persistent over-flagging escalates to R11.
**Severity:** blocking.
**Failure mode:** Schema rejects deliverables with 4+ flags. The Drafter is invoked with `revise` and the instruction: "R13: deliverable has 4 verify flags. Either consolidate flags addressing the same underlying gap, retrieve more material to resolve a flag, or, if the gap is genuinely unresolvable, escalate to R11 LowConfidenceGate by issuing an `incomplete: true` signal in your output."

## Detail

R13 is the "research cliff" rule. It exists because LLMs, when uncertain, default to hedging — and a deliverable full of verify-this-and-verify-that flags is functionally indistinguishable from no research at all. The reviewer who has to verify five separate things would have been better served by an explicit "I can't answer this yet" notice (R11), because at five flags the agent is essentially asking the reviewer to do the research themselves.

The 3-flag cap is a forcing function: the Drafter must either (a) consolidate redundant flags, (b) actually resolve the uncertainty by additional retrieval, or (c) acknowledge that the deliverable can't be produced and escalate. The cap pushes uncertainty out of the deliverable's body and into a clean accept/reject decision.

**What a verify flag is.** A `verify_flag` is NOT a hedge in the prose. Hedges in prose ("the worker *may* be classified as...", "this *could* depend on...") are governed by W3 HedgeDensity, not R13. A verify flag is a structured pointer asking the reviewer to spot-check a specific factual or legal anchor:

```json
{
  "topic": "applicability of § 2775 to per-diem nurses",
  "location_in_draft": "paragraph 3, sentence 2",
  "why_flagged": "The cited case (Dynamex) addressed delivery drivers; the agent's application to per-diem healthcare is by analogy. Confirm this analogy holds against more recent CA healthcare-staffing cases.",
  "suggested_verification": "Search CourtListener for 'Cal. Lab. 2775 nurse OR healthcare 2023..2026' to find direct authority."
}
```

A good flag is specific (points to a sentence), explains *why* it's flagged (what's uncertain), and suggests *how* the reviewer can resolve it (a specific search or document). Bad flags ("verify this") are caught by the schema's `why_flagged.minLength: 50` and `suggested_verification.minLength: 30`.

**When to flag, when to escalate.** The Drafter's decision tree:

- 0 flags: confident HIGH delivery; no uncertainty to surface.
- 1 flag: a minor open question that doesn't undermine the analysis. Fine.
- 2 flags: two distinct open questions. Acceptable but starting to be noisy.
- 3 flags: at the cap. This is the maximum acceptable level of "yes, but check these."
- 4+ flags: too uncertain to deliver. Escalate to R11.

**Consolidation.** Before escalating, the Drafter is instructed to try consolidation: if three flags all stem from the same underlying gap (e.g., three flags about whether AB5 applies to per-diem workers, all because the case-law base is thin), they can be consolidated into one flag covering the gap. This is one revise loop. If consolidation doesn't bring the count to ≤3, escalate.

**Why 3 specifically.** PRD §7 specifies 3 as the cap. The number is empirically derived from how legal-research-tool users behave: reviewers will follow up on 1–3 flags; with 4+ flags, they tend to either reject the whole draft or skim past the flags entirely. 3 is the upper bound where flags are still useful signals rather than noise. Configurable in `extensions/agentic-law-os/r13-config.json` if the team wants to experiment.

**Audit logging.** Every flag, including consolidated and dropped ones, is logged to the audit chain. This lets a quarterly review compare flag-to-revision behavior across the corpus of deliverables.

## Interactions with other rules

- **R11 LowConfidenceGate** is the escalation path when R13 over-fires. R13 → R11 is the canonical "too uncertain" route.
- **W3 HedgeDensity** is the prose-level analog: R13 governs structured flags, W3 governs hedge words in the analysis prose. Both can fire on the same deliverable; they signal different things.
- **R3 NoMemoryStatutes** sometimes forces a flag — if the Drafter can't find a retrieved source to back a particular assertion, the choice is: (a) drop the assertion, (b) retrieve more, or (c) flag it for verification. (c) is the R13 path.

## Test cases

✅ Compliant: 1 verify flag
```
Drafter emits a deliverable with 1 verify_flag pointing to a specific factual uncertainty (the case-law base for an analogy).
Schema: passes (1 ≤ 3).
Result: deliverable rendered. Slack response includes a "Verify before relying:" section with the single flag.
```

✅ Compliant: 3 verify flags (at the cap)
```
3 distinct flags, each addressing a different underlying gap (a fact-pattern fit question, a recent-case-law question, a regulation-vs-statute interpretation question).
Schema: passes (3 ≤ 3).
Result: deliverable rendered.
```

❌ Non-compliant: 4 verify flags
```
Drafter emits 4 flags.
Schema rejects: { revise: true, instructions: "R13: deliverable has 4 verify flags (cap is 3). Options: (a) consolidate redundant flags (flag #1 and #3 both address the AB5-per-diem question — combine?); (b) retrieve more material to resolve one of the flags; (c) escalate to R11 LowConfidenceGate with incomplete: true." }
```

❌ Non-compliant: 5 flags, consolidation insufficient
```
Drafter emits 5 flags. Revise attempt consolidates flags 1 and 3 (→ 4 flags total). Still over cap.
Drafter emits incomplete: true.
Result: R11 LowConfidenceGate fires. Deliverable is replaced with "research incomplete" notice listing the 4 underlying gaps.
```

❌ Non-compliant: flag with no suggested_verification
```
verify_flag.suggested_verification: "" or absent
Schema rejects: { revise: true, instructions: "R13: verify_flag.suggested_verification is required and must be ≥30 chars. Specify a concrete next step (e.g., 'Search CourtListener for ...' or 'Pull CA Code Regs. tit. 8 § 11760')." }
```

❌ Non-compliant: hedge-as-flag pattern
```
Drafter inserts a flag "topic: 'this could go either way', why_flagged: 'the answer is uncertain', suggested_verification: 'have someone double-check'"
Critic flags as a hedge-disguised-as-flag (low-content). Result: revise with instructions to (a) make the flag specific or (b) escalate to R11 if the underlying gap can't be specified.
```

## Open questions

- **Cap tuning.** 3 may be too tight for complex multi-state surveys (which might legitimately have 3 flags per state). Possible refinement: per-jurisdiction flag cap in multi-state deliverables (1 per state, plus 1 cross-cutting flag). Phase 4 design.
- **Flag quality scoring.** The schema currently checks length; quality (specificity, actionability) is checked by the Critic. The Critic could maintain a flag-quality score across deliverables and surface trends. Worth building in Phase 4 if flag quality varies noticeably.
- **Reviewer follow-up tracking.** Slack responses with flags should ideally track which flags the reviewer marked resolved. The next deliverable on the same matter could inherit unresolved flags. This is a matter-management feature; Phase 6+.
